Teaching Philosophy

As an instructor I strive to encourage students to reach their full potential to understand and use the material I teach in my classes. Often students come into my classes with the preconception that physics (and math) are hard and that they are "bad at it" - my goal is for them to believe that they can understand and use physics by the end of the course. One way in which I try to encourage this mindset is to give what is called wise critism - that is when talking to students about their work I try to emphasise what they are doing correctly rather then focusing on what they have gotten wrong.

I try to structure my classes such that for the majority of the class period the students are working on problems in small groups. Math and physics are learned by doing problems, not just listening to or watching someone else talk about the concepts. By having the students work in groups those students who are doing well get experience explaining the concepts (which helps strengthen their understanding) and those that are struggling can have the material explained to them by someone who has a similar background (leading to a more comprehensable explanation). Then, if any group still gets stuck on a problem I am present to lead the students along the process toward the right answer.

It is important while constructing and working with these groups to make sure that the groups work well together and do not lead to a single student doing all the work and then just telling the other group members the answers. Another issue which could arise is for a student to be excluded by a group (due to a language barrier or any other factors). Careful construction of groups (based on responses to a short survey and math assessment given on the first day of class) and a grading scheme made to give points for good group work reduce the chances of these problems occuring.

Teaching Responsibilities

I have held a number of teaching positions at Syracuse University for both the Physics and Mathematics departments. Over the past 4 years I have taught or was a TA for the following classes:

  • MAT 295 - Calculus I
    Fall 2018, Fall 2019
    Recitation TA

  • MAT 284 - Buisness Calculus
    Spring 2019
    Recitation TA

  • MAT 285 - Life Sciences Calculus I
    Spring 2020
    Teaching Instructor

  • PHY 212 - General Physics II
    Spring 2020, Fall 2021, Spring 2021, Fall 2022
    Recitation TA

  • PHY 101 - Major Concepts of Physics I
    Summer 2021
    Primary Instructor

The courses where my role was Recitation TA (MAT 295, MAT 284, and PHY 212) were all large lecture courses. For these courses I was responsible for 2-5 recitation sections. For the bulk of these recitations I would have students work on problems in small groups and I would go around an help them when they got stuck; these worksheets were jointly written by all of the TA's for the course. The two math courses also had short weekly quizzes given at the start of recitations - also jointly written by me and the other TA's and graded by me. Finally I was responsible for helping proctor and grade exams for the courses.

For MAT 285 I have put my role as Teaching Instructor as I was the instructor for one section working under a head instructor. The head instructor prepared the syllabus, selected the textbook, determined the course schedule, and set the homework and I was responsible for all other duties. These included preparing and giving lectures as well as writing and grading tests and quizzes.

Finally, I was the Primary Instructor for the summer section of PHY 101 and was fully responsible for all parts of the course. I wrote the syllabus, selected the textbook, gave lectures, assigned and graded homework, and wrote and graded "quizzams" (an assessment somewhere between a quiz and an exam). This course also has a lab component and I worked with a TA who prepared and administered these labs.
